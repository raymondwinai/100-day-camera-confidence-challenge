The thing you keep putting off

You've known this for a while

You're good at what you do. People feel it the moment they're in a room with you.

But here's the truth: the people who need you are online, and you're not there yet. If you're not top of mind, you're not on their mind at all.

So you've thought about it, maybe for months. But every time you go to start, the advice contradicts itself, the whole thing feels overwhelming, and the camera just… stops you.

So you put it off again.

This is the 100 days where that finally changes, for good.

The design

Why it works

Confidence on camera isn't a talent. It's a habit. And habits are built one way: small reps, repeated, until the thing that once felt hard now becomes the thing you just do, naturally.

But nobody builds a habit on willpower alone. You know this from the gym. You don't get fit by reading about it, you get fit by showing up. And you show up far more reliably when there's a class waiting and people sweating beside you, making the hard thing feel normal.

Train alone at home and you quit in a fortnight. Train in a room full of people doing the same thing, and somehow you keep going.

That's the whole design here.

The reps build the habit. One published video a day.

The group makes the reps happen. A cohort on the exact same days as you, who notice when you go quiet. When motivation dips, and it will, that's what carries you across.

Why one hundred

Why 100 days?

This was never about making 100 videos.

It's about who you become while making them.

On day 1, posting feels like effort, a stretch, something just outside who you are. That's normal: right now, "content creator" is a costume you put on each time.

By day 100, it's not a costume. It's just you.

Somewhere in there the sentence subtly changes…from "I'm trying to post more" to "this is just how I show up now." You stop deciding whether to film. You just film, the way you don't decide to brush your teeth.

That shift is why short challenges don't work. You can't rebuild who you are in 30 days. The research puts the average habit closer to 66 days, often longer. A month-long challenge ends just as the old identity loosens its grip, before the new one sets, and you slip back.

100 days is long enough for the new identity to hold. And you walk away with the proof in your hands: a hundred pieces of content that say, plainly, this is who I am now.

You don't just "finish" this challenge. You become someone.

A bit about me

I'm going to open up a bit here…

I grew up with social anxiety. A proper introvert, the kind who'd rehearse what to say and still freeze. I had trouble going up to the cashier to pay, let alone strike up a conversation. I always felt like, deep down, I had a lot I wanted to share, to help — and I knew the impact one message on social media could have, reaching so many people. But I couldn't talk to people in a room, let alone a camera.

Nobody handed me confidence. I worked for it. I read the books, took the courses, walked into uncomfortable rooms on purpose, again and again, until they stopped being uncomfortable.

Then it clicked: the camera is the same thing. Same fear, same judgment, same quiet worry about whether you're good enough, just with a lens in front of it.

And here's the honest part. I still find it hard sometimes. That's exactly why I can help: I'm not on the far shore shouting that it's easy. I'm a few steps ahead on the same path, and I know where it gets slippery, because I've slipped there myself.

Before you ask

Questions people ask

I'm an introvert. Is this for me?
It's especially for you. I built it as one. This isn't about becoming loud. It's about being yourself, with the fear turned down.
What do I post for 100 days?
You get a prompt every day. The prompt isn't there because you're short of ideas. It's there so "I don't know what to post" can't be the excuse.
I've tried before and stopped.
So has almost everyone here. That's the point. You don't need more willpower. You need a structure, and a group, that holds you when the motivation dips.
How much time a day?
Around fifteen minutes, especially at the start. If it ever takes an hour, something's gone wrong.
What if I miss a day?
A gentle check-in, not a punishment. The streak is there to pull you back, never to shame you.
What gear do I need?
A phone. That's it. We deliberately ignore gear early on. You add it later, once the habit's there.
